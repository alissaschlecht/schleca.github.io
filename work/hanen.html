I came on the Hanen project to help build a new website that would meet their modernization goals as they begin incorporating more technology internally and within products and services.

We worked as an internal team to build a website with best practices, from research and wireframes through to the live version

We started the project by identifying who uses hanen, and why. We conducted user surveys (online and by phone), in-person interviews, card sorting, and user testing of the previous site. From this we generated personas, user scenarios, and experience maps of the current web use.

We established Hanen's core functions in wireframes. Next, we prototyped clickable user journeys based on our three main personas. We tested these on real people to compare success rates and qualitative feedback with the previous website before starting to build HTML templates.

To represent the global reach that Hanen has, we incorporated recurring visual cues to locations. The shapes below are a selection of country illustrations used to represent workshop locations. Because hanen has hundreds, and soon maybe even thousands of workshop locations, we opted for lightweight, scaleable, and locally recognized country shapes instead of city images to decrease future asset creation work for Hanen and keep loading times light.

Small features can be key features:

To make it easier to download items that required agreeing to terms and conditions, and for Hanen to keep track of what resources were being used while saving hours of manpower, we implemented automated agreements.

We started by creating user journeys for each unique agreement process, and creating wireframe prototypes to illustrate the new process, both to test with users and garner approval from the legal counsel. We then generated high-fidelity interfaces utilizing a nearly instantaneous approval process fully on the client side, resulting in immediate downloads for the user and continuous legal protections for Hanen.

Looking to the future:

A big next step for Hanen is growing their resources in online learning. To make these courses easily accessible, we integrated the videos in their training history for easy on demand viewing, anywhere and everywhere.

Learnings: Test smaller, test earlier, keep testing more often.

Despite our best attempts to test prototypes and live code at key intervals on major flows, there could have been more iterations of user testing on key features, especially as business rules changed post-design / post-development and we risked making assumption-based decisions.
