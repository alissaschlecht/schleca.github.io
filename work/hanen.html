Strategy

Defining the Product

The Hanen Centre is a well established non-profit that serves professionals and parents working with children with language and literacy needs.

I came on the project to help build a new website that would meet their mondernization goals as they began incorporating more technology internally and within products and services.

The first phase of the project started with a review of the company and market, the goals of internal stakeholders, and the current information architecture.

  • Grow lines of products & services

  • Decrease pushback against misunderstandings of the Hanen model

Research

Pain Points & Site Review

While many stakeholders had definitive answers to the issues that current users were facing, I wanted to establish a strong baseline of who we were serving, and what they wanted.

We surveyed and interviewed current members (proffessionals) and parents already engaged with Hanen as models of continuous users, and students of Speech Language Pathology and parents (with and without children who might benefit from Hanen's services) not engaged with Hanen as models of new users.

At this point, user testing was conducted on the old website to help establish pain points.

Takeaways: User Experience

  • Brand friendliness / appearance
    Hanen offers resources to everyone equally, but the perspective from many caregivers and parents is that most content is for professionals only, such as ‘member only content’. Hanen has such a huge amount of diversity in it’s products that finding a balance for the entire audience is challenging.

  • Site navigation
    The high level needs of users are clear, but many people struggle to find the information on the website, both due to confusing navigation structures, a missing search feature, and unclear categories for specific subjects.

  • Event registration
    The primary resource that Hanen offers are workshops - multi day, high ticket price, in person events that qualify an SLP to run a program on the same subject to parents. However, the registration, pre and post event experiences were confusing, difficult, and highly manual.

  • Content leads
    One of the primary avenues for new leads to Hanen are through articles found by google searches. Some articles have very high popularity, but almost all have a bounce rate of 85% or more. In user testing, this was backed up when users reported feeling confused on where they were, or where to go next and opted to use the back button to google instead of exploring the site further.

Analysis

Identifying the who (and why, why, why, why, why)

This phase overlaps in many ways with the previous one, but the significance of the outcomes warrents it's own mention.

The Hanen Centre offers many diverse services, and there are many stakeholders working hard to continuously meet the needs of the users of those services. With that hard work comes strong opinions about what users want. A primary goal of this phase was to clearly establish who we were serving, what they wanted, and why they wanted those things - directly from the users themselves.

The creation of personas and experience maps were instrumental later in the project to help refocus on who we were serving, and ask questions based on them, while allowing us to say "I don't know" and work to find better answers.

Design

Defining the Design

At this point, we started to change gears from discovery to design.

I love this phase of a project, because it really sets the tone for the design work after - a balance between a strong foundation of guidelines and freedom to innovate creates a really robust mind-space for deep work.

In terms of process, we were still establishing our production and review mechanisms for design at this point. We started with two week sprints, but quickly found that one week per feature / milestone was more efficient and manageable.

Methods

Mood boards

Brand guidelines

Wireframes

Highlights: Establishing the foundations

  • One of my favourite challenges were working with the brand colours (that had been established before with a logo redesign) to be used accessibly. I ended up darkening the blue so we could use it with white and light gray, and setting strict designations for the secondary orange to only be used for non-essential highlights

  • A major theme of the brand and web identity were clean, proffesional, and friendly - I think this came as a sort of communal reaction to the previous website, which was tight and difficult to read and navigate.

  • The main navigation was a comprimise. While we were still using language that was clearly not connecting well with our users, the business was not ready to update the terminology. As a compromise, we included easy, clear descriptions at the first level of navigation in the mega-navs.

Sidenote

Value guidelines

The team established these values based on the first phases of research, and we would refer to them often, from brainstorming to reviews.

Accessibile
A primary goal of this website was to be future-thinking and inclusive. Making it accessible was a foundation of each design.

Easy
The average user for the Hanen website is incredibly diverse, and we needed to make a one-size-fits-all product. The relative expanse of options and potentially confusing complexity of the organisation itself cemented the need for simplicity and an MVP mindset for easy and clear experience on the website.

Guiding and Supportive
For many users, especially parents and caregivers, the topics they are researching can be challenging, complex, and emotionally taxing. Being helpful speaks to the core mission of Hanen.

Professional
Hanen strives to maintain a well-respected and trustable reputation, and it was imperative to embody this in all aspects of design.

Production

Iterate

This is the place that our sprints truly started to shine in all their glory. We had worked out a lot of the kinks, easily jumped into whatever role we could help with, and had a clear and helpful review process.

Because we were such a small team, all wearing so many hats, I really wanted user testing to be a main component of our design cycles - so that we could spend more time listening to what did and din't work, and less time in the murkey waters of our own opinions. It was a lofty goal, and often difficult to include in every iteration, but it is one of the things I think helped us stick to time and functionality goals, and gave the product a fighting chance at being helpful to it's users.

Key Features

  • One of the known future issues was that Hanen would never have a major budget for updates. With this in mind, I adopted the mindset of reuseability, so that many different sections could reuse the same types of components.

  • We tried to incorporate as much user testing in this section as possible. Not everything when through a testing cycle with users, but we utalized the office staff as much as possible with A/B testing and user testing with InVision flows, and reached out to willing members and parents when we had big questions or high stakes features.

  • I used a mixed set of tools for these prototypes, depending on what we needed to do. The bulk of everything (wireframing to high fidelity) was created in Figma, and some were built as HTML templates for presentations and testing.

Production

Build

As the design process was very underway, we had a hiccup with our development team, and lost our front-end. After months of struggling to replace the position, I volunteered to start building some of the designs out in HTML, with our backend developer there to start incorporating them in a CMS. The process ended up working really well, and more efficiently in terms of time to production. Unfortunately, my time in design, especially testing, took a signficant hit and a few features will need to have extra attention at release. Our real goal was to release something in a reasonable amount of time, and I think that goal is being met.

Status
